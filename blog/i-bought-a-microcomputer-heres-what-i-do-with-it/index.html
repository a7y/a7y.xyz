I bought a microcomputer. Here's what I do with it

If you’re reading this blog, you’re surely familiar with the RaspberryPi, a ~$30 microcomputer. Only, I didn’t buy the RaspberryPi, I bought the ODROID-C1 by HardKernel for $35.

The C1 has 4 cores, generously provided by the Amlogic S805 SoC with ARMv7 architecture, and 1GB of DDR3 RAM. As opposed to the RaspberryPi B+, which is also $35, having only 1 core on ARMv6 architecture and 512MB of DDR2 RAM.

Obviously this is an enormous performance boost for no price difference at all. What’s the downside? The RaspberryPi is much more widely supported than any other microcomputer on the market. The Linux kernel actually incorporates a number of improvements affecting the RaspberryPi. This is more than can be said about HardKernel’s products and other RaspberryPi competitors.

Fortunately, this downside doesn’t really matter to me. My plan was to set up a non-critical homeserver, hence, I went ahead and got the most bang for my buck. And although I’ve only had the unit for about a week, I’m very happy with my decision.

HardKernel allows you to purchase a microSD card (or, alternatively, an eMMC module) with either Android or Ubuntu preinstalled. Android wouldn’t have been a good choice for what I wanted to use the C1 for, and I’m not a fan of Ubuntu. However, I am a huge Arch Linux fanboy.

The independent ALARM (Arch Linux ARM) project was organized to support systems containing an ARM CPU, such as the RaspberryPi and the ODROID-C1.

Even as an Arch fanboy, I would be the first to tell you to not use Arch Linux in mission-critical situations. Just like most other rolling-release distributions, it’s simply too unstable to be used in an uptime-dependent context.

But as I said previously, uptime is not an enormous liability for the type of usage I had in mind.

I currently serve data over 3 different protocols with my C1:

NTP - Every computer in my house now has its clock synchronized with eachother, down to a fraction of a second. The C1 supports an optional RTC (real-time clock) battery which made the board a perfect candidate for serving time.

HTTP - I’m debating getting rid of this because I don’t use it often, but I currently serve an instance of Dokuwiki to use personally. It sounds useful as hell, having your own personal wiki to document everything.

The problem is that I’m too lazy to actually document anything. Furthermore, my usage of Trello makes the wiki almost a redundancy.

DNS - I use a package called Dnsmasq to cache and serve DNS queries. Even though lookup time is boosted by more than 100%, this process only decreases the total connection time by, on average, 100ms.

The real advantage to doing this, however, is the fact that I can set up locally resolvable domain names. For example, wiki.local resolves to the wiki mentioned above.

After a week of ownership, I am incredibly happy with the product and its performance. I’m glad to say that ALARM and the board itself have both been working flawlessly. And with only ~80MB of memory being consumed, I have plenty of room for expansion.

I’ll be sure to write a new post if I do anything else worth mentioning with the C1. I highly recommend the board if you have a similar use-case.
